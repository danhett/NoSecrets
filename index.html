BBC R&D - PRIMER / 'NEARLY LIVE'

Primer was a piece of research into creating a completely new workflow, that would allow the BBC to cover more events more cheaply - it used four massively high resolution and completely unmanned cameras to record a live event, and allowed a single operator to create and jump between virtual HD cameras defined from this huge wall of pixels. Really what this project did was create a whole new sort of craft role, between cameraman, director and editor, all in real time.

BBC R&D - PRIMER / 'NEARLY LIVE'

The 'nearly live' aspect came in when the operator was able to stop time - you set yourself a buffer window between the event happening and being broadcast, which allows you time to make fine-tuned edits and do things like blanking swearing out etc - some things have to be really live-live (like a football match) but for something like say, a music festival, even a few minutes of time to make decisions results in a hugely improved transmission quality. And, this is unmanned cameras and a single operator, so the costs are massively reduced, allowing the BBC (or anyone) to cover more events. This whole front end system was built using browser tech, which was a fairly intense technical undertaking but resulted in something that actually didn't even need to happen on site - I edited a live transmission from the Edinburgh festival while sitting hundreds of miles away in my office in Manchester.

Oh, and...

A gallery in my home city was about to close, and move to a new huge space - they came to me and asked if we could spend some time re-interpreting some of their materials into new forms. This included tons of architectural plans, data, and some amazing typography, which is what we focused on.
We chose to not focus on the final pieces we designed, but actually tried to illustrate the process. Using a quick but brute-force algorithm, we created a process that would try, over hundreds of thousands of iterations, to reassemble a smal number of polygons into the finished form, as closely as possible. The code itself ended up being a THROWAWAY TOOL, and wasn't the product - the print was the product. This is important to me - as a programmer I'm often instinctively very protective over my code, my sources. But actually more often than not, it's fine to trash it once it's served it's purpose!

And so, what do I actually mean when I talk about “transparency” and “openness”? I guess this is important to clarify, given I'm about to scale all this up beyond just what I'm up to.

TECHNICAL TRANSPARENCY

From a technical standpoint, this is easy - where possible, like so many others that I work alongside, I open source my work under highly permissive licenses. I utilise open source technologies and platforms. These very slides are written in Javascript, the images andd code distrubuted under a completely permissive license (more on that in a moment). Learning this stuff has never been more easy - and this has been driven enormously by the open sharing of ideas. It blows my mind, still, now, that anyone with any piece of garbage computer and an internet connection can learn almost anything. And, more excitingly still, this also means that anyone with a piece of garbage computer and an internet connection can SHARE everything too. And that's what makes this whole thing tick. It's what makes me tick.

CREATIVE TRANSPARENCY

From a creative standpoint, I follow a very open process too. We are all products of our environment and our experiences and our fellow human beings - I don’t think you get the best results as a creative practitioner by locking yourself in a basement and working alone. I prototype often and early, put them in front of real people quickly, iterate publically, and share the process. And I think this one is perhaps harder to do. It's harder to shift your mindset than it is to shift where you stick your code. But, it can be done.

THIS FEELS WRONG!

In many ways, all this seems counter-intuitive. Sharing what you make while you’re still making it? Giving your code away, that you worked so hard on? But it isn’t counter-intuitive. It does make sense. By working this in way, we enrich practice, we enrich our work, we enrich ourselves.

WIN #1

TEST QUICKLY, ON PEOPLE WHO AREN'T ME/COLLEAGUES

I get to test things I’ve made, but most importantly: on someone who isn’t me. This gives me wholly unexpected results, and steers the thing I’m making in a direction I never would have landed on.

WIN #2

FAIL FAST, GET FEEDBACK, ITERATE REPEATEDLY

Sharing a prototype I’ve built very early on, publically, and having it fail. I reach a conclusion that would have taken much longer (or potentially never being found) had I kept it to myself.

WIN #3

RELEASE THE SOURCE, WATCH THE MAGIC

I can release the source code for a project that I’ve deployed, and then another programmer, much more skilled than I (there are many!) forks the code, makes a swathe of improvements, and I pull them back into my stream of work. Everybody wins.

FAIL BOLDLY

And so this brings me up to now: I’m a creative coder and digital artist, and I work completely openly in all respects - from my designs to my code to my data to my conclusions, even if the thing I’m working on fails. Oh and by the way, one of the other really nice side-effects of working quickly and transparently is that failure is a PERFECTLY acceptable endpoint. If you work quickly enough that it doesn't matter, failure becomes a learning experience. Or even a required step. And if you're working with others and sharing those mistakes? Failure becomes a jumping-off point for new directions, new voices, more input.

THIS ISN'T ABOUT ME...

OK. So you’re getting the transparency thing. Great. But, this isn’t about me, is it? This is about you too! This is about us as a community of creators - designers, builders, makers, experimenters. This is about us as consumers, and users, and human beings.

...IT'S ABOUT YOU

But it starts with you. And doing it is easy. Open source that side project. Publish your prototypes, your research. Engage with others. And not just when you succeed! The only thing you do by keeping this stuff to yourself is limit the potential for good in your work.

DEFAULT TO TRANSPARENCY

In other words - transparency can and should be your default operating mode. And what happens is, that you start to reap all the benefits that I outlined today as a single person. But then, it grows: transparency cultivates a positive culture shift for teams, technologies, companies, whatever.

BE BRAVE

You just have to be brave. Being transparent with your work, your mistakes, your path - it is brave. Doing anything (building software for example) a certain way should never ever be justified by saying “that’s how we’ve always done it”. Ever. Transparency and open process requires a shift in thinking, completely.
And so, what I think is happening - almost imperceptibly at first, but now very rapidly - is that I think we’re seeing a shift in the ownership and steerage of our technology. We're developing an awareness as consumers and owners of technology. Like, my parents now comment on this stuff in at least a slightly informed way, and that can only be good, right? We’ve got a long way to go, but I feel like it’s happening. We’re starting to understand, as a community of users and people, that giant opaque entities owning our data and driving our experiences is ultimately a really bad thing. And we, right here, are the designers and gatekeepers of the systems that are powering this shift.
